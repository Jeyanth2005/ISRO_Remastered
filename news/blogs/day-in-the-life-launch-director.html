A Day in the Life of a Launch Director

ISRO Insights | September 30, 2025

The final countdown is the most visible part of a rocket launch, but for the Launch Director, it's the culmination of months of meticulous preparation and a day of intense focus. The Launch Director for the recent PSLV-C58 mission provides a rare glimpse into the controlled chaos and immense responsibility of launch day at the Satish Dhawan Space Centre.

My day begins long before sunrise. The 'day' of the launch is really the final 24 hours of a much longer process. By the time I arrive at the Mission Control Complex, the launch vehicle is already fully fueled and the teams have been running pre-flight checks for hours. My first task is to preside over the Mission Readiness Review meeting. Here, the heads of every team—propulsion, avionics, tracking, weather—give their final status report. It's my job to synthesize this information and make the ultimate 'go' or 'no-go' decision at each critical stage.

"In the control room, there is a sense of calm, focused energy. You trust your team, and you trust the process. My voice authorizes the launch, but it's the chorus of hundreds of dedicated engineers and scientists speaking in unison. Every 'go' you hear is backed by thousands of hours of work." - Launch Director, PSLV-C58 Mission

The hours leading up to T-0 are a steady stream of data. I am constantly monitoring telemetry from the rocket and the satellite, communicating with the range safety officer, and keeping a close eye on the weather. The final 15 minutes are the most intense. The automatic launch sequence takes over, and my role shifts to monitoring. We are watching for any anomaly, any parameter that deviates even slightly from the norm. When the clock hits T-0 and the engines ignite, there's a moment of awe, but our work isn't done. We monitor the vehicle's ascent, the separation of each stage, and finally, the successful deployment of the satellite into its orbit. Only then, when the satellite is confirmed to be healthy and on its own, can we finally breathe and celebrate another successful mission for India.
