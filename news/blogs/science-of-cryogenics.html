The Science of Cryogenics: Fueling Our Future

ISRO Insights | September 18, 2025

Cryogenic engines are the powerhouses behind ISRO's most powerful rockets, like the LVM3. They provide the high energy needed to launch our heaviest satellites and propel our most ambitious interplanetary missions. But what exactly is cryogenics, and why is it so essential for space exploration?

Cryogenics is the science of producing and behaving of materials at extremely low temperatures. In rocketry, it refers to the use of propellants that are liquefied and stored at temperatures below -150°C. ISRO's cryogenic engines use a combination of liquid oxygen (LOX) as the oxidizer, stored at around -183°C, and liquid hydrogen (LH2) as the fuel, stored at an incredibly cold -253°C.

"Mastering cryogenic technology was a game-changer for India. It gave us self-reliance in launching heavy satellites and unlocked the potential for missions like Chandrayaan and Gaganyaan. It's a highly complex field, but the performance payoff is enormous." - Senior Propulsion Engineer, LPSC

The main advantage of cryogenic propellants is their high efficiency. Rocket performance is measured by 'specific impulse,' which is essentially the amount of thrust generated per unit of propellant consumed. The combination of liquid hydrogen and liquid oxygen provides the highest specific impulse of any chemical rocket propellant in use today. This means more thrust for less fuel, allowing us to build more powerful rockets and launch heavier payloads.

However, this performance comes with immense engineering challenges. Storing these super-cooled liquids, developing turbopumps that can operate at thousands of RPMs in these extreme temperatures, and ensuring the controlled combustion of these propellants is an incredibly complex task. ISRO's successful development of its indigenous Cryogenic Upper Stage (CUS) for the GSLV and the powerful CE-20 engine for the LVM3 is a testament to the dedication and ingenuity of our engineers, placing India in an elite group of nations to have mastered this critical space technology.
