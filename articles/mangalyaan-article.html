Mission Objectives and Achievements

The primary objective of Mangalyaan was to demonstrate the technological capabilities required to design, plan, and execute an interplanetary mission. The mission was successfully placed into Martian orbit on September 24, 2014. It was designed for a lifespan of just six months but continued to operate for nearly eight years, a testament to ISRO's robust engineering. It provided a wealth of scientific data and over a thousand breathtaking images of the Red Planet.

Scientific Discoveries

Mangalyaan's instruments studied the Martian surface features, morphology, mineralogy, and atmosphere. The Mars Colour Camera (MCC) provided a stunning atlas of the planet, while the Methane Sensor for Mars (MSM) placed crucial upper limits on its atmospheric methane concentration, a key data point in the ongoing search for life. The mission also provided valuable insights into the atmospheric escape processes, helping scientists understand how Mars lost much of its water.

Scientific Payloads of Mangalyaan

Payload Full Name Objective
MCC Mars Colour Camera To capture images and provide context for the other instruments.
TIS Thermal Infrared Imaging Spectrometer To map surface temperature and mineralogy.
MSM Methane Sensor for Mars To measure methane in the Martian atmosphere.
MENCA Mars Exospheric Neutral Composition Analyser To study the neutral composition of the Martian upper atmosphere.
LAP Lyman Alpha Photometer To measure the relative abundance of deuterium and hydrogen, to understand water loss.
